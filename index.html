    Vehicle Transfer in Ontario

    Situation of Concern

    As part of the Ontario Digital Service in partnership with the Ministry of Transportation, we were tasked with investigating the private vehicle transfer problem space in Ontario. The overall goal was to determine the major pain points of the current process to provide insight for the migration of this service to a digital medium. Understanding that which users currently enjoyed about the process would also lend to a better online design in the future.

    Understanding the Problem Space

    We began by researching the current process in order to become experts at how the transfers are currently performed. In doing so, we identified three cases for users in this process; buying a used vehicle, selling a used vehicle, and transferring a vehicle as a family gift. We whiteboarded out each of these processes step-by-step to gain a solid grasp as to how the sequence plays out. Having done so, we recruited interviewees that had performed any of the three tasks to gain a greater level of insight regarding the situation. By conducting user interviews in-person or with videoconferencing software, we were able to hear about first-hand experiences and take notes on how they felt about the overall vehicle transfer journey.

    Building the User Journey Maps

    The final design created in figma settled on showing 5 main areas (steps, touchpoints, attitudes, pain points, and costs) in combination with a line graph that clearly represented the user's emotions at each stage. This allowed the clients to easily understand where pain points occurred in the process.

    Buyer Journey Map

    The main pain point identified when buying a used vehicle privately was just after purchasing the vehicle. Such sales would typically be done at the person's home, where the buyer would take off their plates and hand off the keys. At this point, the car would not yet have a temporary license plate for the new owner. This would leave the buyer in a position where they would need to drive the car without plates or tow it to a Service Ontario centre to obtain these plates, leaving them stressed and nervous.

    Seller Journey Map

    The biggest pain point with regards to the seller's journey is the lack of clarity with regards to the responsibilities of the seller vs the responsibilities of the buyer at the beginning of the transfer process. Knowing who needs to safety the vehicle, purchase a transfer package, or when to offer a test drive would be incredibly useful to these users. Currently, the Ontario government does not provide a source online that guides the user through step by step, leaving the user confused and anxious when selling.

    Family Gift Journey Map

    The family gift process was identified to be fairly simple, with few highs or lows throughout. The one pain point identified was that there is a lack of clarity regarding who can sign the affidavit for the transfer; while some answers are available online, the answers obtained at physical Service Ontario locations differ. This difference leads to confusion and distrust among users.

    The Electric Share

    "The Electric Share" is a local multiplayer battle arena game created in under 48 hours during the G.I. Games Institute 2018 Game Jam by our Game Jam Team "Spaghetti Yeti". Following the game jam theme "Dynamic", the game was designed with the idea of constant motion and change in mind. Each player controls a living spark of electricity.

    The game was developed entirely in Unity. All art assets were generated within the 48 hours from scratch. These included a level background, main-menu art, platforms, player characters, and projectiles. Using Adobe Illustrator and Photoshop, all frames were hand drawn using tablets. The project was ambitious given the time limit, but the end product was genuinely fun to play with little to no bugs. Using Git as a version control system, the team was able to work cohesively on different aspects of the project and routinely bring it together for testing. Efforts payed off, as it was deemed "most fun" and "most on-theme" of all participants.

    UW RoboHub Logo Design

    The University of Waterloo's RoboHub is a new robotics research and testing facility in need of a logo. They desired a timeless, memorable recognizable design that was simple and clean even from a distance. After establishing some general themes (technology, innovation, togetherness of a hub, robotics), I proceeded to sketch out as many ideas as possible.

    Many early iterations heavily relied on imagery and designs taken from the robots currently a part of the RoboHub fleet. While this is on-theme, it takes away fromt he timeless nature of the logo. Thus, a more broader concept was realized; a robotic heart.

    The idea of a heart captured the concepts of a "hub" while also tying together elements of humanity and technology (one of the main goals of robotics). The minimal "low-poly" aesthetic conveys the artifical nature of the structure, as well as the addition of cogs to add to its mechanical nature. Overall, I am pleased with the result, although perhaps the robotic elements of the cogs could have been done more creatively. The final asset was created using Adobe Illustrator.

    The Electric Share

    "The Electric Share" is a local multiplayer battle arena game created in under 48 hours during the G.I. Games Institute 2018 Game Jam by our Game Jam Team "Spaghetti Yeti". Following the game jam theme "Dynamic", the game was designed with the idea of constant motion and change in mind. Each player controls a living spark of electricity.

    As players move, they generate electricity which can be used to release greater and greater blasts of energy upon opponents. However, these blasts don't hurt them necessarily; they charge your opponents up! Players die by being "overcharged", which occurs when a blast of electricity puts them over their voltage limit. Be careful shooting indiscriminately, or your opponent just might send your own charge right back at you. The last spark standing (or running) is the victor.

    The game was developed entirely in Unity. All art assets were generated within the 48 hours from scratch. These included a level background, main-menu art, platforms, player characters, and projectiles. Using Adobe Illustrator and Photoshop, all frames were hand drawn using tablets. The project was ambitious given the time limit, but the end product was genuinely fun to play with little to no bugs. Using Git as a version control system, the team was able to work cohesively on different aspects of the project and routinely bring it together for testing. Efforts payed off, as it was deemed "most fun" and "most on-theme" of all participants.

    Central User Directory U.I.

    The Ontario Institute for Cancer Research utilizes the Central User Directory is an administrative tool for group management and coordination. The tool contains multiple features for managing memberships groups as well as individual users.

    Together with the UI/UX designer as a part of the OICR-WebDev team, a completely overhauled administrative user interface was constructed. Mockups were produced in Photoshop, and versions were coordinated with members through the use of inVision. The tool itself was built using HTML, SASS, and Bootstrap using Jekyll as a static site generator.

    The final version displays a cleaner design with a more intuitive workflow. Notifications and warnings important to the core users (such as expiration and pending approval) are more clearly displayed, and navigation has been improved through a new navigation bar. A new colour scheme gives the design a neater appearance and a stronger brand-identity.

    The interface structure has been completed, but still requires implementation. Thus, it has not yet been integrated into the current central user directory.
